Ark alien innovations have profoundly altered the world of Kassatím, though these changes remain uneven. Assembling Ark alien technology often demands rare, exotic resources, which limits mass production and restricts its widespread use. Furthermore, the process typically requires specialized factories and workshops. As a result, while Ark alien technology is relatively common, it has not wholly transformed the world. Kassatím, therefore, presents a highly incongruous aspect: high-speed, marásh-equipped naval vessels mingle in harbors with traditional sailing ships, while edifices reinforced with advanced metallic alloys retain their pre-Ark architectural simplicity. While no food is scarce due to genetically enhanced crops, basic cereals like wheat and barley remain dietary staples. The cities are illuminated with electrical skeins; meanwhile, much of the countryside remains untouched by artificial light. And while Ark technology may have revolutionized warfare, many human warriors still cling to their bronze swords rather than adopting plasma torcs. In essence, Kassatím is a stark blend of antiquity and modernity: the deeply archaic and the astonishingly advanced.

Humans and Science

In Kassatím, Ark alien sciences and their resultant technologies have been eagerly embraced by most cultures, including Qeret. Consequently, the sciences and their related professions have become integral to society. However, while most Ark aliens may be forthcoming regarding scientific knowledge, most humans still struggle to fully comprehend such intricate subjects. As a result, human "experts" in specialized fields frequently possess only a rudimentary grasp of the fields they study. For instance, a Qereti physicist may understand antimatter as the fundamental opposition to regular matter but would typically lack any deeper insights. Nevertheless, some human scientists are quicker to grasp such topics, often proving to their professional advantage.

Advanced Agronomy

Of all the Ark alien sciences, Advanced Agronomy has altered human civilization more than any other. Once, famine and drought provided an endless source of anxiety for any human village, city, or empire. Luckily, such whims of nature no longer threaten the world. Farmers now use fertilizer pods to quickly enrich the most barren tracts of land. Talented geneticists have partnered with agronomists to breed unique varieties of wheat and barley that are hardier, more nutritious, and disease resistant. Other inventions are subtler, such as the use of enriched heavy water that sinks deep into the fields. And yet others more noticeable, such as the massive, pivoting silver reflectors that provide additional sunlight onto the wheat and barley fields of the world.

Within Qeret's Ark Sector lies the Greenery, a sprawling greenhouse dedicated to advanced botanical experimentation and breeding operations. Among its most popular innovations are fungal orbs, used by many for their narcotic properties and augmentative effects. By splicing mushrooms with traits from diverse Ark organisms — including various algae, bacteria, plants, and other exotic lifeforms — the botanists of the Greenery have engineered mushrooms capable of inducing temporary epigenetic changes in human cells upon consumption. Orbs might boost physical endurance, bolster the immune system, or heighten cognitive abilities, depending on the variety consumed. These augmentations are accompanied by vivid psychoactive effects, including euphoria, mania, or sanguinity. Each orb, roughly the size of a large bead, is designed for convenience: to induce the desired effect, one merely crushes the orb in hand and quickly inhale the scattered spores.

Ark Weaponry

War-chariots once ruled the battlefields of Kassatím, often serving as the decisive factor in military engagements. Highly mobile, quick, and devastating at range, war-chariots could end most battles conclusively or deter them altogether. With the arrival of the Ark, this paradigm of war endures, but the instruments have changed. Victory now belongs to the nation that deploys the greatest arsenal of Ark weapons to the battlefield.

Perhaps most destructive, and most sought after, is the fell particle hammer. These weighty, expensive, and cumbersome devices are capable of disintegrating thousands of combatants in mere moments. When activated, these leviathans of titanium and pulsating plasma conduits release blinding shafts of beta particles woven with gamma rays. Impact from a particle hammer’s roiling violet light initiates a near-instantaneous cascade of atomic disintegration as particles burrow into molecular bonds, rending them with ease. Excluding Knakros, the accumulation of particle hammers is a top priority for the six Great Kingdoms of Kassatím. Though, despite the rising number of such devices — some estimates place the total around 10,000 — the Great Kingdoms have thus far exercised unprecedented restraint, as particle hammers have yet to be deployed in battle. Many advocates for peace across Kassatím have called for the dismantling of each and every particle hammer in existence and an inter-kingdom proscription on their use and construction.

Less destructive, but equally concerning, are the diminutive devices known as Pahl-drones. Developed on behalf of, and named for, the Combassyian God-King, Banipahl Byzes, these insect-sized machines primarily function as near-undetectable modes of surveillance. Each comes equipped with microneedles capable of delivering toxins, both lethal and non-lethal. In Combassyia, Pahl-drones are ubiquitous. They flit through the streets of Scintevah, indistinguishable from the native midge, constantly relaying reams of data back to the royal palace and the scornful eyes of Byzes’ intelligencers. Beyond Combassyia, use of the Pahl-drone is controversial owing to concerns regarding privacy. The Kingdoms of Knakros, Léu, and Netusha refuse to use them on principle, while Makaria and Drys deploy them only situationally and sparingly.

Another notable Ark weapon is the plasma torc: a high-energy metal band that snugly wraps around the arm. Constructed of heat-resistant, superconducting alloys, the torc channels highly ionized gas and projects focused plasma arcs that vary in intensity, depending on the needs of the wearer. Short, weak bursts may be used to disperse crowds and may, at worst, result in mild burns, while intense arcs may reach several thousand degrees, allowing them to burn easily through armor and flesh. Most soldiers, regardless of kingdom, wear a plasma torc around their upper arm; these are often painted with the colors of their respective nation.

While Ark technology has undoubtedly altered the way humans conduct war, the weapons of the past still remain widely in use. Swords, spears, and bows are still used by warriors, mercenaries, criminals, or any others prone to violence. Not merely for obvious matters of cost, but for matters of sentiment as well. Weapons are, among other things, objects of culture. And humans, perhaps more than most species, cleave to their cultural past. Much like fashion and architecture, human warfare has become a tapestry that seamlessly spins the past and the future, the alien and the human. That said, such archaic weapons are frequently augmented with Ark technology. Such novel syntheses include vibratory bronze swords coated with acidic nanite gels, liquid-nitrogen arrow tips that freeze on impact, shields that release gravitic pulses, etc. The variety of such weapons is nearly endless, as they are often designed by specialist blacksmiths on an ad-hoc basis for individual clients.

Atelonics

The scientific discipline of Atelonics examines the fundamental particles that constitute matter, their interactions, the forces that govern them, and the mechanisms by which energy is transferred or exchanged.1 The achievements of this field extend far beyond incomprehensible theoretical musings, however; it has spurred numerous essential innovations that have enhanced Kassatím’s infrastructure, refined its communication networks, and vastly improved its management of energy resources within its urban centers.

Following the Ark’s arrival, the vessel’s vast array of antihydrogen drives was harvested, transported, and retrofitted to power each of the major cities of Qeret. These antihydrogen power plants not only meet but vastly exceed the energy demands of entire cities. At their core, the plants produce trace amounts of antihydrogen, an antimatter counterpart to hydrogen that annihilates upon contact with regular matter. Magnetic containment fields prevent the antihydrogen from interacting prematurely, ensuring controlled and immensely energetic reactions. From these plants, skeins — needle-thin electronic pathways embedded in urban infrastructure — channel power generated by these antimatter reactions throughout the city for lighting, heating, cooking, and scientific experimentation (see Macrotech).

Ark alien atelonicists, supplemented by precocious human researchers, have also extended their efforts across the electromagnetic spectrum and beyond. This includes magnetic railways to transport goods and passengers at incredible speeds,2 plasmatic forcefields capable of creating localized zones of protective, super-heated plasma, and the terrifying particle hammers — a devastating class of Ark weaponry (see Ark Weaponry). Of particular note are Kezamir engines, which extract zero-point energy from vacuums to generate propulsion without fuel. Although prohibitively expensive to manufacture, these engines are expected to revolutionize terrestrial and aerial travel once mass production becomes viable. Currently, most naval vessels rely on the more accessible water-powered márash systems (see Macrotech).

Another groundbreaking achievement is the operational use of entangled particles. Leveraging some particularly baroque artifacts salvaged from the Ark, macroform scientists in Makar have engineered long-distance communication devices known as relays. These devices enable the instantaneous transfer of digital and verbal information between cities, bridging vast distances faster than the speed of light. However, their exorbitant cost restricts their use to city leaders and the immensely wealthy.3 Despite this limitation, the relay has prevented (and exacerbated) numerous economic and military conflicts since its introduction.

Genomics

Genomics is the science and practice of editing human, animal, and plant genomes. Within several decades after the Ark’s arrival, the Ark Aliens had successfully mapped and decoded the genomes of most native creatures in Kassatím, including humans. Shortly thereafter, they introduced the delicate art of Genomics to the human population. Using ancient techniques perfected aboard the Ark, genomicists are able to make broad, fundamental, and inheritable alterations to any living creature.4

Their first major contribution to humanity was a “cocktail” of fortified genes that rendered humans resistant to disease and illness; effectively tripling the average lifespan of humanity overnight. This triumph was soon eclipsed by the advent of antisenescence treatments, a genetic innovation that drastically retarded the aging process and, theoretically, enabled human immortality. This has naturally led to an explosion in the human population of Kassatím and the average age now rests well above 100. In fact, it is not uncommon to meet elder citizens who yet recall a time before the Ark, having received antisenescence treatments after its arrival.

For those that cannot afford expensive antisenescence treatments, there are less efficacious renewal treatments that merely revert the body to a younger state; such treatments are generally offered freely at facilities known as Resurrection Temples. In the unfortunate event that an individual’s life is cut short — whether through fatal mishap or murder — death is not necessarily final. By submerging bodies within pods known as resurrection creches, the dead may be retrieved. Once a body is submerged, nutrient-rich and synthetic amniotic fluids — in addition to many swarming dust nanites — work to vivify damaged tissue and carefully restore cellular functions and synaptic patterns. However, this does not come without a cost. When resurrecting the dead, resurrection creches extensively reverse the age of the body (in a manner akin to renewal procedures), meaning that an individual temporarily returns to having the body of an adolescent. This period of youth is accelerated, but patients must spend a month in the Temple, attended by specialist physicians and priests, until their body has returned to its original age.

Most major cities of Kassatím host Resurrection Temples, ensuring their services remain geographically accessible to most of the human and Ark alien populations. While antisenescence remains a privilege of the affluent, Resurrection Temples will typically provide their renewal and resurrection treatments free of charge.

Macrotech

Macrotechnology, or simply “macrotech,” is a broad classification for all computerized technology constructed from components larger than the Dust nanites utilized in the related field of nanotech. Perhaps the most widespread of these devices is the Ark tablet. In shape, these thin, rectangular slabs recall the iconic clay tablets once used by humanity for accounting purposes and literature. Functionally, however, they surpass mere information storage. Friends and families may maintain close correspondence; shopkeepers compile tabular inventories; prudent scientists perform rudimentary virtual experiments to refine hypotheses; visual artists use them to draft precise sketches; and architects leverage their computational capabilities to create structurally sound blueprints before construction of a project even begins. Ark tablets are mass-produced in prosperous cities like Qeret, and — excepting the truly destitute — most citizens carry one at all times.

Another ubiquitous device, and equally vital, is the prompter: a language-translating machine embedded in the ear that converts an interlocutor’s speech into the user’s native tongue. During the Long Century of Integration, prompters were indispensable, bridging the linguistic divide between humans and the newly arrived Ark aliens. Additionally, they have facilitated effortless communication among Kassatím’s diverse human ethnicities. Beyond the odd hermit or rural asectish, nearly every inhabitant of Kassatím, human or otherwise, possesses a minuscule silver prompter implanted within their ear canal (or biologically analogous orifice).

Most urban environments in Kassatím, including Qeret, are laced with thin, ultra-conductive copper cables known as skeins, whose primary function is to transfer power directly from an antihydrogen powerplant to the rest of the city. Instead of a single wire, skeins use braided copper strands alloyed with silver to maximize conductivity. These softly glowing skeins are embedded into the walls, roofs, and streets of a city. For this reason, architects often use them creatively to limn palaces, temples, and market areas with shimmering light. Everything from a structure’s central heating to its lighting are powered by the city’s elaborate skein network, though, the poorest sectors often fall outside of this energy grid.

Holographic projecting devices known as lanterns are exceedingly popular among the Qereti. Matte and oblique-cone-shaped, these unassuming devices fulfill two primary functions. First, they emit scanning rays of light that capture three-dimensional activities. Second, they project holographic recordings of those same events, rendered in user-programmed colors, known as “hololoops.” In Qeret, lanterns are utilized for both commercial purposes —such as holographic shop signs, elaborate advertisements, and product displays — and artistic endeavors, including the recording and projection of traditional Adonian dance performances.5

Among the most useful macrotech innovations have been the mechanical attendants known as servitors. These semi-sentient automatons have freed the population from performing the drudge work and menial tasks that every city depends on, but few relish actually doing. Garbage collection, sewer work, factory labor, etc., are all accomplished by the omnipresent servitors. Not only can one see servitors in the streets of Qeret, but also in the many Qereti establishments known as bachnals, where they serve food and drink (and mop up the occasional puddle of retch). Most well-off Qereti families own at least one servitor that functions as a servant, with some even serving as personal assistants and constant companions.6 While each servitor is typically custom-built, the standard model is a lanky, child-sized automaton distinguished by its beady eye receptors.

Of particular importance to the Qereti is the water-powered engine known as the marásh. These sophisticated machines propel Qereti naval vessels across the Cerulean at high speeds. Their design utilizes water as both fuel and propellant by harnessing heat and pressure to generate thrust. Seawater is drawn into the marásh reservoir, where it is rapidly heated by sunlight concentrated through a specialized lens. The resulting high-pressure steam is expelled through a narrow nozzle at the ship’s stern, producing forward momentum. Trained navigators adjust the nozzles and valves to regulate steam output, allowing precise control over speed and maneuverability. Naturally, the hulls of marásh-equipped vessels must be reinforced to withstand the increased velocity. By enabling rapid transit over the sea, the marásh has revolutionized naval trade, particularly for the coastal cities of Kassatím such as Qeret.

Microtech

Microtech refers to the science of, and engineering of, microscopic, computerized machines referred to as nanites, or simply “Dust.” Extending a mere 60 microns in length, a single “grain” of Dust is barely perceptible to the human eye, and clouds of them resemble dusky, floating masses of dirt (hence the moniker). Though expensive to manufacture, and resource intensive, these nanites accomplish many crucial tasks. For instance, they help maintain structures, ships, and tools by cleaning, polishing, and performing small repairs on metal or woodwork, making them invaluable to artisans and shipwrights. They are also employed in Resurrection Temples where they are used as healing aids capable of treating wounds or even reconstructing flesh, whole cloth. Resurrection creches, in particular, rely on the slow, but effective, process of using Dust to reform damaged or missing flesh.

Dust is often used to augment agronomic practices as well. Capable of combating pests, creating shade, and purifying water, it is not uncommon to see ominous clouds of Dust swirling around the Qereti wheat and barley fields on the mainland. Some cartographers and adventurers use pouches of Dust as exploration tools by dispersing them over unfamiliar terrain to map regions and detect obstacles. This practice serves traders and sailors as well, who use Dust to scan coastlines and rivers. Some painters have employed it as a means to stretch artistic boundaries by creating finer details, intricate patterns, and curious textures that the human hand — or analogous Ark alien appendage — could not otherwise achieve. These nanites can even create moving displays or surfaces, adding a dynamic, shimmering quality to a piece. And more frivolously, Dust often serves a decorative role by adorning clothing with shifting and temporary patterns that symbolize rank, accomplishment, or merely reflect garish personal taste. Though common, one may not always be aware of the Dust’s presence. Whether it is toxin detection, air quality control, water purification, building maintenance, etc., these nanites work without end, contributing to the urban environments of Kassatím perhaps more than any other technology.

In Qeret, many citizens have been enhanced with Dust-infused tattoos known as Ark works. This creative fusion of biotechnology, nanotechnology, and cultural artistry has become a relatively common sight in the city. Ark work dye is derived from a variety of natural sources such as plants, minerals, and even sea creatures (as is the case with the native murex snail, which naturally produces the hue known as Qereti Violet). These dyes serve as a base before Dust nanites are suspended within the ink and finely powdered electrum is mixed in, enabling the ink-Dust solution to interact with the body’s innate bioelectricity.

Once applied, Ark work tattoos can perform a variety of augmentative functions. For instance, by connecting directly to the body’s nervous system, an Ark work may temporarily enhance an individual’s reflexes, strength, or perception. They may also act as conductive pathways, allowing the wearer to store external energy (e.g., from skeins), only to be released as explosive bursts of strength or speed. The tattoos can work reactively or by deliberate neural signal. One might intentionally trigger a burst of adrenaline before a casual foot race, or the Ark work might reflexively lower the wearer’s heartrate when they are faced with anxiety. Ark work activation does not come without cost to the wearer, however. Such unnatural manipulations of the body’s biochemistry take their toll, and one must generally contend with fatigue and mental exhaustion after activating one’s Ark work.

Ark work patterns and designs are typically influenced by Qereti cultural motifs. Wavy lines symbolize the sea and can frequently be seen on the biceps of sailor’s arms and on shipwright’s hands. Depictions of Nasiru, tutelary god of Qeret, often adorn the flesh of the pious, and baroque portraits of the Ark are exceedingly common as well. Ark works can be acquired from specialist artist-technicians throughout the city, though the most reputed shop for the procedure is known as the Blackworks, located in the Ark Sector.

Spectrography

A significant hurdle in utilizing Ark tech within such a primitive environment is the scarcity of resources. Each of these technologies, along with the factories used to construct them, require precious metals and materials that were previously inaccessible — and, in many cases, entirely unknown — to humans before the Ark. Some of the most advanced examples, including relays, antihydrogen power plants, and particle hammers, consist of parts that must be harvested directly from the Ark itself, a deceptively endless spring. However, for society to develop new devices independent of the Ark, it must secure reliable sources of exotic materials. While the framework of a laboratory or factory can be fashioned from limestone and cedar, the intricate machinery within demands cobalt, osmium, bismuth, quartz, and more. Thus arises the need for Spectrography.

Spectrography is a broad term encompassing the tools and techniques used to procure both rare and common resources essential to the modern world of Kassatím. Deep-probing spectrometers, commonly referred to as assessors, scan vast expanses of land, mountain, and seabed, swiftly identifying deposits rich in precious metals. Once a deposit is detected, heavy boring hammers — closely related to the particle hammer (see Ark Weaponry)— carve a path to the prize. The extracted materials are then recovered by durable mining servitors, engineered to withstand extreme pressure and inhospitable conditions. This entire process often unfolds without direct human or Ark alien oversight; government officials or Ark alien supervisors simply issue directives and activate these automated mining operations, known as “spectro sites,” in designated locations. Currently, human society has minimal regard for environmental conservation, and there is little concern for the effects of extensive mining on the rural regions of Kassatím. However, prudent Ark aliens involved in such operations will generally avoid culturally significant landmarks, such as Mt. Betta on the Adonian mainland.
