Before the Ark

The world of Kassatím is old and its history reaches back thousands of years. Proto cities first emerged in the southern alluvial plains of Makaria, and impelled by an extraordinary agricultural revolution, these cities began to proliferate and appear across the many regions of Kassatím. At these nascent sites early peoples erected monolithic structures as tributes to ancient gods, and the written script, crestform — employed by imprinting crescent shapes into wet clay — was developed to track trade and the ever-rising surpluses of barley, wheat, and precious metals. In tandem with these developments, rigid social hierarchies arose, and temple administrations took great care to control the flow of resources and to efficaciously tax the growing urban populations. High priests positioned themselves at the apex of these hierarchies, though in time they were replaced by kings — often deities in their own right. This stratification of society became commonplace as agricultural, artisanal, commercial, and scholarly professions continued to emerge.

As agricultural advancements disseminated, so too did the practices of writing and monumental construction. Many of the other major urbanized cultures in Kassatím adopted the crestform script and adapted it to their respective tongues. In Makaria, great ziggurats dominated the skylines and served as the central administrative and religious hubs of each respective city. Outside the Léusian hamlet of Ixia, five immense limestone pyramids encased in marble were erected in the desert in tribute to their divinely appointed kings. As the number of cities steadily increased, the world became a greater patchwork of budding nations and great wonders.

Eventually, following centuries of countless wars and territorial concessions, six great regional powers emerged in Kassatím: Makaria, Léu, Combassyia, Netusha >, Knakros, and the runt of the six, Drys. At the same time, Adoniah emerged as a loosely connected network of culturally linked yet politically independent city-states. Extensive trade routes linked these Kingdoms and their vassals, creating a vast interconnected world of flourishing commerce and culture. Progress, however, is relative. For little did the myriad people of Kassatím know, their many accomplishments were soon to be dwarfed.

The Ark

The Ark’s origins are shrouded in mystery. Propelled by a silent, unnamed, unseen, and inscrutable intelligence, this gargantuan space vessel traveled through the galaxy for uncounted eons. However, the Ark was not aimless, as it often directed itself to worlds that stood at the brink of disaster. Supernovas, apocalyptic wars, rampant disease, among myriad other calamities, often leave civilizations on the verge of annihilation. It was at these times, as civilizations faced their impending destruction, that the Ark would appear, waiting to accept the refugees who sought to live another day. Through countless cycles, the Ark embraced hundreds of alien species from across the galaxy. It graciously accommodated these wretched beings, supplying not only a home, but all the necessities each required to live. Its motivations and purposes, however, remained elusive. To this day the Ark has never spoken on such matters — assuming, of course, it is capable of speech or even thought.

There came a time, however, when the Ark’s endless cycle of retrieval and nomadism came to an end: the day it arrived on the planet Earth. The Ark, seemingly finished with its mission, landed at the center of the Cerulean Sea upon the isle of Dactos, off the coast of Knakros. Its advanced systems shut down, permanently it would seem, and opened itself to the Edenic world outside. After untold generations aboard the Ark, its inhabitants were eager to explore this new world. And that they did. In the years since, Ark aliens have settled in every city, town, and hinterland of Kassatím.

What does the Ark look like?

The Ark is black as onyx, though the composition of this dark alloy remains a mystery. It stands vertically, reaching eight kilometers into the sky, making it the tallest peak in the known world. As such, the higher one navigates its labyrinthian halls and stories the less natural oxygen one can breathe. At its rectangular base it is two kilometers wide. On most days only its bottom half may be seen, as the rest towers well above the clouds. Its structure is non-uniform, with concavities, convexities, and sharp angles running up its length. On a clear day the Ark can be seen from Qeret and many other major cities along the coast of the Cerulean. Looming dark in the distance, the Ark is a foreboding sight indeed.

In the years since its arrival, the wildlife and plant life of Dactos has begun to encroach into its cavernous and dormant interiors. Despite its current desolate state, the Ark still attracts a steady stream of tourists. The few people that call the Ark home are scholars who seek to riddle the true nature of the Ark and its inscrutable pilot.

The Long Century of Integration

In the century following the arrival of the Ark, a long period of Ark alien migration and integration followed. This period was not fraught with war, as you might expect. The Ark aliens largely came in peace and were generally accepted by humanity. Initially, many perceived the Aliens as deities. This should come as no surprise, considering the Ark aliens descended from the skies and wielded technology that appeared assuredly divine. Nonetheless, in time, it became apparent that these new arrivals were not gods or angels or spirits, but simply other living beings — albeit exceptionally exotic living beings. Further, it was the material offerings of these Ark aliens that most ingratiated them to their hosts, as to turn away these migrants meant turning away their gifts as well.

As such, the most immediate and dynamic change that the Long Century of Integration brought to Kassatím was not the introduction of the Ark aliens themselves but rather their advanced technologies. Their tools and sciences were as useful as they were spectacular: micron lasers that could slice and carve marble in a fraction of the time it would take to chisel; fertilizer pods that could replenish entire swaths of barren land; bionic implants known as prompters that enabled instant cross-cultural and inter-species communication; particle physics that allowed entangled communication across vast distances. Rapidly the world of Kassatím became woven with threads of Ark alien technology.

The product of this union is a magnificent amalgam of these two disparate mega-cultures. Entire Makarian ziggurats are laced with highly magnetic skeins that allow them to float serenely in the sky. Humans walk the streets of Lux with neural implants that enable them to witness announcements descended from the great democratic machine-pyramids of Lux. Promptly, the wedging of clay tablets with crestform became an obsolete method of record keeping, as digital Ark tablets allowed information to be stored virtually and indefinitely. Further, Ark alien philosophies and ideologies have caused an unprecedented shift in the popular imagination, as secularist, existentialist, and even anarchist ideologies have become accepted by a growing number of humans. It was a renaissance of culture and science on an unprecedented scale. Now, the comparatively primitive humans are like children in a sandbox of wonder, though their understanding grows with each passing generation and in increasingly greater strides.

Naturally, the scholars, priests, and scientists of Kassatím resolved to begin a new calendar with the Ark’s arrival, marking its inauguration as Year 1, A.A. (After Ark). Scholars have since agreed that the subsequent Long-Century ended when an ichor — known to most as simply Zet — ascended the throne of Netusha in the year 112 A.A.: a historic occasion that signaled an end to the era of integration, and the start of a new age of seamless human and Ark alien cohabitation.

Present Day

It is now the year 284 A.A. and roughly ten human generations have passed since the arrival of the Ark. Most scholars agree that Kassatím has entered a golden age. Starvation and famine have ceased to be a concern for the majority, as advanced agronomic technologies have created unprecedented surpluses of staple cereals, ensuring that nearly all are well-fed. Ark alien medicine has eliminated most major diseases and no longer do cities worry over seasonal plagues and unseen ailments. While day-to-day survival and subsistence had once been a chief concern for most, now the noble causes of exploration, development, and progress excite the imaginations of millions.

However, though civilization may thrive, not all is well in Kassatím. Warfare persists, as the introduction of advanced Ark alien weaponry sparked a perpetual arms race that dominates the concerns of most leaders. Internal social and political revolutions loom over most major cities, as Ark alien knowledge has caused many to question the validity of religious and governmental control. Factions — major and minor, local and global — vie for control of the many cities, kingdoms, and trade routes of the world. The world of Kassatím teeters on the edge. Will the cultures of Kassatím transcend and become another of the great galactic civilizations? Or will it be consumed by its own unchecked ambition?

A History of Adoniah

In addition to the Great Kingdoms of Kassatím, there is the disunified assemblage of city-states known as Adoniah. Adonian cities such as Qeret, Taath, and Erutu share common origins, and though independently governed, also share a common religious pantheon, language, architectural style, and mode of dress. A highly fractious culture, Adoniah does not compare to great kingdoms such as Léu and Combassyia in terms of military power. Rather, Adonian cities exercise economic influence through trade and cultural influence through the arts. Adoniah is a merchant culture grown rich by its trade routes across the Cerulean, particularly those that bring goods from Drys, Léu, and Knakros to Netusha and the Twin Rivers. Adonian cities such as Qeret and Erutu serve as locus points of trade that enable each of the great kingdoms, in turn, to thrive commercially, making these Adonian sites crucial elements in the international trade-network of Kassatím.

The Adonian people emerged in the southern alluvial plains of Makaria as a distant cousin to the Makarians. From the outset, the Adonian people were industrious and insatiably curious. Eschewing war and conflict, the Adonians leaned towards commerce. Following a widespread exodus — spurred by the calamitous and widespread salinization of their native land — the Adonians migrated west, eventually settling in the coastal region now known as Mainland Adoniah. It was then that the first Adonian city, Qeret, was founded, though the Erutians claim this milestone as well.

In the centuries following, the Adonians began to innovate culturally. They were the first culture to develop an alphabet: the most notable alternative to the ancient logo-syllabic script known as crestform. While crestform remains the dominant form of writing in Makaria, Combassyia, and Netush — and the ever-iconoclastic Léusians juggle no less than three unique forms of writing — the Adonians and other Cerulean cultures such as Drys and Knakros cleave to the alphabet. The convenience and relative simplicity of the alphabet enabled those traditionally excluded from literacy to begin reading, writing, and corresponding. As a result, a growing class of merchants arose: industrious and relentlessly entrepreneurial, this upstart sphere of Adonian society only expanded following the arrival of the Ark. With new sought after goods, and Ark-enhanced tradeships, Adoniah's economic dominance was solidified, forever centering the merchant estate in Adonian culture.

Several hundred years before The Ark’s arrival, the Adonians went through an extended age of colonial expansion. The five cities of Mainland Adoniah founded “daughter cities” across the Cerulean, which, collectively, are referred to as Colonial Adoniah. Most famous of these colonies is the city of Taath, daughter of Qeret, which lies within the borders of Léu. Qeret and Taath are closely allied despite residing within the borders of different kingdoms. Though the cities of Colonial and Mainland Adoniah are far flung, the intricate network of shipping lanes used by the Adonians ensures a constant exchange of both information and goods. Indeed, the Adonians are known the world over for their immense trading fleets that tirelessly ply the Cerulean, connecting the Léusian, Dryic, and Knakriti markets to the eastern half of the world.

Even before the arrival of the Ark, Adonian society had been among the most sophisticated cultures of the Cerulean. Owing to numerous technological advancements and lucrative trade routes, Adoniah became a diverse, urbanized, widely literate, and highly affluent civilization. Indeed, the ubiquity of Adonian commerce positively impacted the rest of the world as well. Till the emergence of Adonian cities like Qeret, naval networks of trade remained limited in scope, often restricted to regional neighbors. It was not until the Adonian era of exploration and trade that the great kingdoms formed an interstate system of trade, diplomacy, and even war. This economic dominance has persisted since the Ark, as Qeret has taken particular advantage of Ark-derived propulsion systems known as maráshes that send its trading vessels skimming across the waters of the Cerulean at stunning speeds. But though Adoniah thrives commercially and technologically, it has never been a unified political entity. Tethered by custom rather than leadership, Adonian city-states compete economically while sharing a common sense of cultural identity.

Very rarely has Qeret, or any of the Adonian city-states, been a truly sovereign nation. Despite Adoniah’s influence, its cities remain relegated to the perpetual status of vassal — owing perhaps to Adoniah’s very disunity as a political entity. Over the centuries Qeret has changed hands between Léu and Combassyia many times over. Each, in turn, demanding stiff tribute and undying loyalty. At the present, Qeret owes fealty to Combassyia and its emperor Banipahl Byzes, a fact that many of the Qereti chafe at.
