The arrival of the Ark ignited profound political transformations across Kassatím. Before its arrival, human societies largely adhered to long-standing social structures rooted in tradition. Concepts like social stratification, taxation, slavery, tributes, and war were as perceived natural and immutable; political beliefs, beyond religious notions of divine social order, were scarce. The advent of the Ark, however, ushered in a centuries-long reckoning. For the first time, humanity began a sustained critical assessment of its dominant modes of governance and control. This upheaval gave rise to varied responses across Kassatím. While divine right remains a legitimate basis for rule in kingdoms such as Combassyia, new systems of governance have emerged elsewhere.

In the Great Kingdom of Léu, a digital direct democracy has replaced the divinely appointed Nesurai, enabling citizens to participate directly in their nation’s future. Netusha, once ruled by militaristic tyrants, now answers to a pious and pacifistic Ark alien king, whose influence has fundamentally altered the kingdom's conduct in matters of war and peace. Knakros has embraced a science-driven holacracy, decentralizing power in favor of expertise and collective decision-making. And within Qeret, too, radical ideologies have sprung that challenge the established social order. These movements threaten to unravel the city’s hierarchies in a historic cascade that could abolish slavery, depose the king, and sever the yoke of Combassyian rule.

Izabel's Rapture

Within the wretched streets of the Slave Sector resides a young freedwoman known only as Izabel. Each evening, after the day’s drudgery, Izabel speaks to all who will listen. She envisions a future where slavery is eradicated, and war is pruned from human experience. Though no longer owned by any master, Izabel still bears the Ark works that once marked her servitude. Serene yet charismatic, she draws to her cause the bonded and freed alike. This burgeoning movement, dubbed Izabel’s Rapture, is the fastest-growing political force in Qeret, drawing the attention of many far beyond the Slave Sector, including some of the city's wealthiest denizens.

Unsurprisingly, this movement terrifies the slavers of Qeret, whose wealth and comfort depend on the steady trafficking of human chattel. In an effort to extinguish this incendiary cause, they have imposed strict curfews and meted out violent punishments to those caught consorting with the Rapture. Some have even offered Izabel great sums of silver to abandon her mission; she refuses such overtures graciously and with elegance. As her influence swells, gossip of Izabel’s Rapture fills the streets. It is rumored that the matter has reached the ears of King Tiraal himself, who wishes to resolve the matter decisively — one way or another. Izabel has established herself in a spacious villa within the Slave Sector, funded entirely through donation. Known as Izabel’s Haven, this sanctuary hosts her nightly lectures and offers refuge to all — bonded or otherwise — who seek solace and guidance.1

The Seditionists

Less harmonious than Izabel’s Rapture, but endowed with significantly deeper coffers, the insurgent group known as the Seditionists seeks to overthrow the Qereti monarchy under King Tiraal. A well-organized and clandestine faction, they differ from the Unfettered in one crucial respect: they do not seek to abolish governance, but to establish an independent Qereti state — one free of vassalage to any of the Great Kingdoms. Composed of Qeret's most rarefied elite, including aristocrats, oligarchs, merchants, and elite scholars, the Seditionist movement represents a curious fusion of revolutionary fervor and patrician self-interest. While many citizens chafe under Combassyian oversight, the Seditionists aim not merely to cast off vassalage but to enthrone Qeret as a sovereign power among the Great Kingdoms. While their rhetoric may invoke principles of liberty and self-determination, their true aspiration is the unshackling of trade. A relentless combination of taxes, tariffs, and an archaic tributary system has too long interfered with the business interests of Qeret's wealthiest citizens. Whether through radical policy whitepapers, or spirited debates in Dryic-style bathhouses, the movement's central dogma remains clear: Qeret must rule itself, or it will be ruled by ledger and leash.

To advance their aims, the Seditionists wield not only wealth and rhetoric but violence and backroom coercion. In recent months, a string of targeted attacks has wracked the city. Among them, the wholesale destruction of a Combassyian tariff office in Libnaan’s Bounty, and the obliteration of the eastern wing of the Combassyian Embassy in the Spice Sector — both, it is whispered, executed with illicit, perhaps experimental, particle hammers. Most chilling of all, a Combassyian diplomatic entourage was murdered in their sleep within the Royal Palace itself — an act so brazen it all but announces complicity at the highest levels. The Seditionists, unrepentant, refer to such spectacles not as crimes or excesses, but as deeds of conviction.

Faced with such threats, King Tiraal’s response has been, put in the most generous terms, quite tepid. His reluctance stems from a fear of antagonizing Seditionist members, powerful Qereti citizens each.2 Meanwhile, the King faces mounting pressure from Banipahl Byzes to crush the Seditionist movement or face the "strictest sanctions." King Tiraal now performs a precarious balancing act as he attempts to preserve his throne, avoid war with Combassyia, and forestall outright rebellion. Meanwhile, the Seditionists envision a Qeret no longer subservient to Banipahl Byzes, and they intend to achieve this goal with or without their King’s assent. Historically, such coups have merely resulted in a formal “changing of hands,” with Qeret transferring its vassalage to another Great Kingdom such as Léu or Makaria. The Seditionists, however, consider this potential outcome unsatisfactory. Rather, they dream of a Qeret totally free of foreign rule: self-governed, self-determined — a state the city has not experienced in centuries.

Technologists

The Technologists, a radical reformatory movement born in the Great Kingdom of Knakros, seek salvation not through kings, parliaments, or ideologies, but through the inscrutable minds of the macroforms. They hold that only by surrendering society's fate to these algorithmic oracles - whose arcane calculations unspool probabilities with chilling precision - can true progress be attained. Though, in reality, macroforms are loathe to divulge such insights, the Technologists persist, exalting the macroforms as the last unimpeachable arbiters of future's course. Their dream is one of abdication: a world freed from fallible judgement, shepherded into an Edenic future by unerring and discerning computer-minds.

When the Ark arrived 284 years ago, it landed upon the Knakriti isle of Dactos. As such, Knakros became the first of the Great Kingdoms to parley with the Ark aliens. The ensuing influx of alien immigrants ignited a feverish and enthusiastic period of assimilation for the Knakriti. Indeed, today Knakros boasts the highest proportion of Ark aliens in Kassatím — a culture now known as much for its restless innovation as its zeal for social experimentation. From this crucible emerged the Technologists, a coalition of eccentric scientists who, by the year 142 A.A., had grown to view humanity’s self-rule as a foolish anachronism. Left unattended, they argued, mankind would collapse beneath the weight of its own appetites. Only the macroforms and their guarded equations, they claimed, could avert such disaster. In the Technologists' vision, all governance, all diplomacy, all labor of conscience, would be surrendered to these oracular machines; even the sovereignty of the individual, they warned, must someday give way.

Although the macroforms are outwardly flattered by such trust, the vast majority reject this vision as neither wise nor necessary. They strongly recommend humanity to rely on its sense of self-preservation and to learn from its mistakes. Moreover, they emphasize that their probabilistic predictions are constrained to the short term — they can forecast the weather a week in advance, for instance, but not a year. The further into the future an event lies, the more variables proliferate, each branching into further contingencies, until the resulting tangle of possible futures grows so vast that accurate long-term predictions become computationally infeasible. Despite this, the Technologists remain skeptical of the macroforms’ professed limitations.

Most major cities host a conclave of Technologists. In Qeret, they gather at the Technosanctum, a solemn edifice tucked within the Spice Sector near the Knakriti Embassy. There, under the stewardship of Sasara — a charismatic, if erratic human visionary — they convene weekly councils to strategize and refine their doctrines. Sasara herself is a familiar figure in the city’s public life: staging rhapsodic lectures in the Nonagon, petitioning King Tiraal within the royal palace to embrace "the wise macroformic algorithms of tomorrow," and forever seeking the imprimatur of the city's macroform community. Technologists are a common sight in Qeret’s streets, often found in fervent conversation with macroforms, attempting to coax from them glimpses of futures that lie shimmering just beyond mortal reach.

The Unfettered

During the Long Century of Integration, many humans were surprised to learn that numerous Ark Aliens did not adhere to notions of sovereign rule and law. A few, like the kemptorans — whose intricate bureaucracies recalled human statecraft — and the asectish — too familiar with the degradations of despotism — possessed experiences that human minds could, if not embrace, at least comprehend. Most Ark aliens, however, hailed from worlds untouched by doctrine, law, or sovereignty. On such worlds, existence unfolded without kings or courts, shaped instead by custom, instinct, and the organic negotiations of communal life. Initially, this disparity of experience posed little more than a passing curiosity, but in recent years, the issue has resurfaced with unsettling force.

Across Kassatím, movements advocating universal emancipation have begun to stir. Though differing in composition and method, they share a singular conviction: that governance is neither a universal constant nor a natural good, but a mechanism of oppression fashioned by elites to maintain their dominion. In Qeret, the most conspicuous manifestation of this creed is the Unfettered — a revolutionary coalition determined to wrest the city from the monarchy’s vise grip. Through clandestine action and a persistent subversion of the law, they seek to dismantle the pillars of hierarchy, depose King Tiraal, and erase the Qereti monarchy from history.

The Unfettered move quietly through every tier of Qeret’s society, from the dockside poor to the wealthiest courtiers. Unfettered membership is a capital offense in Qeret and is punishable by death, which has driven its members to favor a loose and cellular organizing principle. Even so, rumors endure of a hidden figure — Vaar-Toh — believed to serve as the movement’s guiding hand. The monarchy’s chief enforcer, the Impireesh Marble, has made their eradication a personal crusade. Of late his campaign has grown indiscriminate, sweeping up political rivals under suspicion of Unfettered collaboration. He refers to the movement as a "lawless shadow society," and will go to any lengths to root out Vaar-Toh and his "lunatic dogmatists."
